# Research: Algorithmic Feeds and Attention Economics

Research conducted April 2026. Sources prioritise academic research, institutional reports, and major publications from 2022-2026.

## Algorithmic Feeds and Information Quality

How do algorithmic feeds affect information quality and public discourse?

### Milli et al. — Engagement, User Satisfaction, and the Amplification of Divisive Content on Social Media (2025)

- **Author:** Smitha Milli, Micah Carroll, Yike Wang, Sashrika Pandey, Sebastian Zhao, Anca D. Dragan
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://academic.oup.com/pnasnexus/article/4/3/pgaf062/8052060
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** algorithmic-feeds, ad-driven-wellbeing
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** An algorithmic audit of Twitter/X showing that its engagement-based ranking algorithm amplifies emotionally charged, out-group hostile content significantly more than a reverse-chronological baseline. The study finds that users themselves report wanting to see less of this divisive content, revealing a structural misalignment between what engagement metrics optimise for and what users actually value.

### Germano, Gomez, and Sobbrio — Ranking for Engagement (2025)

- **Author:** Fabrizio Germano, Vicenc Gomez, Francesco Sobbrio
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0047272726000253
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** algorithmic-feeds, platform-economics
- **Access:** paywalled (preprint available via Barcelona School of Economics)
- **Annotation:** A theoretical and empirical analysis showing a fundamental trade-off: weighting engagement signals such as likes and shares increases platform engagement but also increases misinformation and polarisation. Provides evidence that Facebook's 2018 "Meaningful Social Interactions" update contributed to increased ideological extremism, and proposes an "engagement tax" as a policy mechanism.

### Corsi — Evaluating Twitter's Algorithmic Amplification of Low-Credibility Content (2024)

- **Author:** Giulio Corsi
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1140/epjds/s13688-024-00456-3
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** algorithmic-feeds
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Using 2.7 million posts on COVID-19 and climate change, this study measures how Twitter's algorithm treats low-credibility content. It finds that tweets sharing information from low-credibility domains perform better in the algorithmic feed than those that do not, with the effect concentrated among high-engagement, high-follower accounts. High-toxicity and right-leaning content sees heightened amplification.

### Liu et al. — Short-Term Exposure to Filter-Bubble Recommendation Systems Has Limited Polarization Effects (2025)

- **Author:** Naijia Liu, Xinlan Emily Hu, Yasemin Savas, Matthew A. Baum, Adam J. Berinsky, Allison J. B. Chaney, Christopher Lucas, Rei Mariman, Justin de Benedictis-Kessner, Andrew M. Guess, Dean Knox, Brandon M. Stewart, et al.
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2318127122
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** algorithmic-feeds
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** An important counterweight to algorithmic panic. Four experiments with nearly 9,000 participants show that manipulating YouTube's recommendations to create filter bubbles and rabbit holes has no detectable short-term effect on political attitudes. The authors argue the burden of proof has shifted for claims about algorithm-induced polarisation. Essential for a balanced evidence base, though it does not address longer-term or non-attitudinal effects.

### Yang and Gonzalez-Bailon — More Platforms, Less Attention to News? (2025)

- **Author:** Tian Yang, Sandra Gonzalez-Bailon
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/14614448251341496
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** algorithmic-feeds, journalism-economics
- **Access:** paywalled
- **Annotation:** Analyses news exposure across TV, web, and YouTube for roughly 55,000 panellists over 39 months. Finds that television still dominates news consumption at a ratio of more than five to one over online sources, complicating narratives about algorithmic feeds replacing traditional media. Provides the empirical grounding for understanding where people actually encounter news.

## Attention Economics

Attention as a resource, cognitive effects of the attention economy.

### Heitmayer — Second Wave of Attention Economics (2025)

- **Author:** Maxi Heitmayer
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://academic.oup.com/iwc/article/37/1/18/7733851
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** attention-economics, platform-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Proposes a model for understanding attention as a currency that can be accumulated and exchanged, distinguishing between "calcified" attention (stored metrics such as follower counts) and "flow" attention (real-time engagement). Updates the theoretical framework of attention economics for the social media era, moving beyond Simon's original scarcity framing to model how platforms monetise and redistribute attention.

### Sharpe and Spooner — Dopamine-Scrolling: A Modern Public Health Challenge (2025)

- **Author:** B. T. Sharpe, R. A. Spooner
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/17579139251331914
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** attention-economics, ad-driven-wellbeing
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Distinguishes "dopamine-scrolling" from doom-scrolling as a distinct behavioural pattern driven by variable reinforcement schedules rather than anxiety. Identifies the pursuit of novel, entertaining content and rapid platform switching as a reward-mechanism-driven habit with public health implications. Published in Perspectives in Public Health, making it a useful bridge between tech criticism and health policy.

### Yousef et al. — Demystifying the New Dilemma of Brain Rot in the Digital Era (2025)

- **Author:** A. M. F. Yousef, A. Alshamy, A. Tlili, A. H. S. Metwally
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3425/15/3/283
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** attention-economics, ad-driven-wellbeing
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** A rapid review examining "brain rot" — Oxford's 2024 Word of the Year — as cognitive decline from overconsumption of low-quality digital content. Synthesises research from 2023-2024 linking doomscrolling, zombie scrolling, and social media addiction to emotional desensitisation, cognitive overload, and psychological distress. Useful as a survey of the current clinical and psychological research landscape.

### Hari — Stolen Focus (2022)

- **Author:** Johann Hari
- **Year:** 2022
- **URL:** https://stolenfocusbook.com/
- **Type:** book
- **Topics:** attention-economics, ad-driven-wellbeing
- **Access:** paywalled (book purchase required)
- **Annotation:** A New York Times bestseller that identifies twelve structural causes of the attention crisis, from tech design choices to pollution and diet. Hari's central argument is that attention collapse is systemic, not a personal failing, and that solutions must be collective — including regulating companies whose business models depend on capturing and holding attention. Accessible entry point for non-specialist readers.

### Wu — The Attention Merchants (2016)

- **Author:** Tim Wu
- **Year:** 2016
- **URL:** https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/234876/the-attention-merchants-by-tim-wu/
- **Type:** book
- **Topics:** attention-economics, platform-economics
- **Access:** paywalled (book purchase required)
- **Annotation:** The foundational history of attention as a commodity, tracing the business model of "free content in exchange for attention, sold to advertisers" from penny newspapers through radio, television, and into the internet age. Wu, a Columbia law professor who coined the term "net neutrality," shows how every new medium eventually becomes an advertising platform. Essential historical context for understanding why today's internet works the way it does.
