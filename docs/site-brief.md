# fundyourinternet.org — Site Brief

> This is the foundational document for the project. It was written before
> any code and captures the original vision, positioning, and design intent.
> Refer to this when making decisions about content direction, tone, or scope.
> For technical decisions made during implementation, see `design-decisions.md`.

## Project overview

A standalone public-good site that empowers non-technical people to take an intentional, positive approach to sustaining the things they value online. It is not a product site for at.fund. It is a resource, a practice framework, and a gateway to deeper understanding — built by the at.fund team, disclosed clearly but not centred.

The relationship to at.fund: "built by the at.fund team" in the footer. at.fund appears in the site only where it naturally belongs — as one tool among several in the alternatives/movement branch. The site's credibility depends on it being genuinely useful independent of at.fund.

## Strategic context

### The problem we're addressing

Most people value things online they've never paid for and could: journalists, podcasters, open source tools, newsletters, feeds, community moderators. The obstacle isn't unwillingness or broken payment rails. It's cognitive overhead — too many platforms, no coherent picture, no sense of agency.

The existing conversation around this problem is dominated by two unhelpful framings:

1. **Tech-solutionist**: "A new protocol/token/payment rail will fix this." Eyes glaze. Alienates the very audience that needs to act.
2. **Generic creator advocacy**: "Support creators!" So vague it means nothing. Every platform already says it.

### Our positioning

We land between those — framing funding as a **personal practice**, not a cause or a product. The analogy is the local food movement: people adopted a legible personal behaviour (shop at the farmers market, know where your food comes from) and the behaviour carried the politics implicitly. Nobody needed to understand supply chain economics. The practice *was* the argument.

### The sequencing thesis

The at.fund project believes the right order is: **legibility → motivation → implementation**. Most projects in this space start with payment infrastructure (implementation) for relationships that don't exist yet. fundyourinternet.org is a legibility and motivation intervention. It helps people see their relationship to the things they use online, and gives them a concrete first action. Payment mechanics are someone else's problem — or a later problem.

### The audience

Engaged but non-technical people. Podcast listeners, newsletter readers, news subscribers, people who use apps and tools made by small teams. They already have funding relationships (subscriptions, one-off tips, Patreon pledges) but don't think of them as a coherent practice. They're not ideologically motivated but they're receptive — they sense something is off about the ad-driven web, they just don't feel empowered to do anything about it.

## Site architecture

The site has a **front door** and **three branches**. The front door is tight, positive, and action-oriented. The branches go deeper — each one earned by the reader's curiosity, each addressing a different need (understanding, belonging, evidence). No branch is prerequisite to any other.

### Build priority

1. **Front door** — non-negotiable for launch, this is the thing people share
2. **Branch 2** (movement/alternatives) — highest emotional resonance, most differentiated
3. **Branch 1** (how feeds work) — important but less urgent
4. **Branch 3** (evidence base) — can start small and grow over time

---

## Front door

### Purpose

Make the reader feel like they have agency they didn't know they had, in under 30 seconds. Not "the internet is broken" — more like "you already sustain things you care about in the rest of your life; you can do the same thing online."

### The core action

The memetic action must be: atomic (one sentence), immediately doable, socially shareable, and positive-identity-forming. It should describe a behaviour that makes the person feel good about themselves without requiring them to feel bad first.

**The quality that makes it memetic**: it's an identity claim, not a guilt trip. "I fund my internet" is aspirational the way "shop local" is — intentional, not sacrificial.

## What this site is NOT

- **Not a movement with a name and a logo and a pledge.** No joining, no membership, no badge.
- **Not a community platform.** No Discord, no forum, no comments section.
- **Not a product.** No accounts, no sign-ups, no data collection beyond basic analytics.
- **Not oppositional.** "The ad-driven internet is broken" is combative. "Fund your internet" is affirmative.
- **Not a content machine.** A small, well-made library that grows slowly is better than a blog with publication pressure.
- **Not a guilt trip.** Every piece of content should leave the reader feeling more capable, not more complicit.

## Design and voice notes

### Voice

- Warm, direct, confident
- Informational without being didactic
- The energy of a well-made independent publication, not a startup landing page
- British English
- No jargon in user-facing content
- No emojis, no exclamation marks in body copy

### Design direction

- Clean, typographically strong, content-first
- Should feel like a public resource or a well-designed zine
- Not a SaaS landing page
- Mobile-first
- Fast-loading, accessible, lightweight
- The three branches should be visually distinct but clearly part of the same site
