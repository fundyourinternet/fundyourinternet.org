# Research: Journalism Economics and Subscription Fatigue

Research conducted April 2026. Sources prioritise academic research, institutional reports, and major publications from 2022-2026.

## Journalism Economics

What is the real economics of independent media and creator sustainability?

### Medill / Northwestern — The State of Local News 2025

- **Author:** Medill School of Journalism / Northwestern Local News Initiative (Tim Franklin, Zach Metzger)
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://localnewsinitiative.northwestern.edu/projects/state-of-local-news/2025/
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** journalism-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** The definitive annual accounting of local news in America. The 2025 edition documents 213 news desert counties (a record), 136 newspaper closures in a single year, and a 45% plunge in web traffic to the 100 largest newspapers over four years. Also tracks the countertrend: over 300 digital news startups in five years, though overwhelmingly concentrated in metro areas.

### WAN-IFRA — World Press Trends Outlook 2024-2025

- **Author:** WAN-IFRA (World Association of News Publishers)
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://wan-ifra.org/insight/world-press-trends-outlook-2024-2025/
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** journalism-economics
- **Access:** registration
- **Annotation:** A global survey of over 240 publishers across 85 countries documenting the structural shift in news revenue. For the first time, print circulation and advertising account for less than half of publisher revenue, while digital revenue has crossed the 30% threshold. Essential for understanding the global picture beyond US-centric data, including the decline of platform partnership revenue from 15.1% to 12% of total income.

### MBO Partners — Creator Economy Trends Report 2024

- **Author:** MBO Partners
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.mbopartners.com/state-of-independence/creator-economy-report/
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** journalism-economics, platform-economics
- **Access:** registration
- **Annotation:** Annual survey tracking 8.9 million independent content creators in the US. The income distribution data is stark: 71% earn under $30,000 annually, only 9% exceed $100,000, and 34% make less than $5,000. Useful for grounding conversations about the "creator economy" in the reality that most participants are earning supplementary income, not a living.

### Pew Research Center — Newspapers Fact Sheet (2024)

- **Author:** Pew Research Center
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/fact-sheet/newspapers/
- **Type:** dataset
- **Topics:** journalism-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Pew's regularly updated fact sheet compiling industry data on newspaper circulation, advertising revenue, and newsroom employment over time. Shows the long-term trajectory: print advertising revenue has fallen from roughly $49 billion in 2006 to a fraction of that, and digital advertising has never come close to replacing it. Valuable as a longitudinal data source even though Pew ceased its full annual State of the News Media report after 2023.

### MDPI — Substack, the New "Home" for Cultural Journalism (2024)

- **Author:** Published in MDPI Journalism and Media journal
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.mdpi.com/2673-5172/6/3/128
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** journalism-economics, platform-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Peer-reviewed academic study examining whether newsletter platforms like Substack represent a genuine alternative media space or reproduce existing journalistic hierarchies. Finds that the most successful newsletters are overwhelmingly authored by already-established journalists, and algorithmic recommendations increasingly shape subscription behaviour. Important for complicating the narrative that platforms alone solve journalism's sustainability problem.

## Subscription Fatigue and Giving Behaviour

What does the research say about subscription fatigue and giving behaviour?

### Reuters Institute — Digital News Report 2024

- **Author:** Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism (University of Oxford)
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/digital-news-report/2024
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** subscription-fatigue, journalism-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Companion to the 2025 edition already in the library. The 2024 report includes detailed data on willingness to pay for news across dozens of markets: only 17% globally say they are willing to pay, 57% will not consider paying anything, and among non-payers just 2% would pay the price of a full subscription. The data suggests the news industry has already captured most of those willing to pay at current prices and formats.

### Deloitte — 2025 Digital Media Trends

- **Author:** Deloitte
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/digital-media-trends-consumption-habits-survey/2025.html
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** subscription-fatigue
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Deloitte's annual consumer survey covering streaming, social media, and digital entertainment. The 2025 edition finds that 47% of consumers feel overwhelmed by subscription management, 75% are frustrated by recurring price increases, and 61% would cancel their favourite streaming service over a $5 monthly increase. Documents the broader subscription fatigue environment in which news competes for wallet share against entertainment.

### Jeong and Lee — Unsubscribing From Over-the-Top (2025)

- **Author:** DongA Jeong, Sang Woo Lee
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/21582440251397865
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** subscription-fatigue
- **Access:** open (SAGE Open)
- **Annotation:** Peer-reviewed study examining the psychological mechanisms behind subscription cancellation. Identifies two distinct factors: "subscription fatigue" (the burden of managing multiple platforms) and "mental coupling" (the constant cost-benefit evaluation users perform). Finds that coupling is the stronger driver of cancellation, suggesting that perceived value relative to cost matters more than sheer subscription volume.

### Tan and Reddy — Sustainable Funding for Small Businesses: Recurring Crowdfunding (2024)

- **Author:** Yee Heng Tan, Srinivas K. Reddy
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/02662426231205758
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** direct-funding-models, subscription-fatigue
- **Access:** paywalled
- **Annotation:** Peer-reviewed study analysing 3,229 crowd patronage projects (Patreon-style recurring funding, as opposed to one-off Kickstarter-style campaigns). Finds that incentivising mechanisms (tiered rewards, exclusive content) have a long-term impact on patronage sustainability, while project characteristics have only short-term effects. One of the few rigorous empirical studies of the recurring funding model that underpins much independent media.

### Wikimedia Foundation — Fundraising Report 2023-2024

- **Author:** Wikimedia Foundation
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://meta.wikimedia.org/wiki/Fundraising/2023-24_Report
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** direct-funding-models
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Detailed data on one of the largest digital public-good fundraising operations in the world. Over 8 million donors across 200 countries gave an average of $10.58 in FY 2023-24, sustaining $168 million in revenue. The declining average donation size (from $11.38 the prior year) and the operation's reliance on banner campaigns in 33 countries and 18 languages provide a real-world case study in both the potential and the fragility of grassroots digital giving at scale.

### Bonifacio, Hair, and Wohn — Beyond Fans: Relational Labour on Patreon (2023)

- **Author:** Ross Bonifacio, Lee Hair, Donghee Yvette Wohn
- **Year:** 2023
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/14614448211027961
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** direct-funding-models
- **Access:** paywalled (New Media and Society)
- **Annotation:** Qualitative study based on interviews with 21 Patreon creators examining the relational labour required to sustain a patronage income. Finds that creators construct relationships with patrons ranging from transactional to familial, and that emotional support from patrons is as important as financial support for sustainability. Reveals the hidden labour costs of direct-funding models that aggregate statistics often obscure.
