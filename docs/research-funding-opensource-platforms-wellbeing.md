# Research: Funding Models, Open Source, Platform Economics, and Ad-Driven Wellbeing

Research conducted April 2026. Sources prioritise academic research, institutional reports, and major publications from 2022-2026.

## Direct Funding Models

What models for direct and community funding are working, and at what scale?

### El Sanyoura and Anderson — Quantifying the Creator Economy: A Large-Scale Analysis of Patreon (2022)

- **Author:** Lana El Sanyoura, Ashton Anderson
- **Year:** 2022
- **URL:** https://ojs.aaai.org/index.php/ICWSM/article/view/19338
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** direct-funding-models, platform-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** The most rigorous large-scale academic study of Patreon, analysing the complete set of pledges exceeding $2 billion from 2013 to 2020. Examines the membership platform from three perspectives — patrons, creators, and the platform as a whole — and finds that patrons who pledge to a narrow set of creators are more loyal but churn off the platform more often. Essential empirical grounding for understanding how direct funding actually works at scale.

### Goldman Sachs — The Creator Economy Could Approach Half-a-Trillion Dollars by 2027 (2025)

- **Author:** Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/the-creator-economy-could-approach-half-a-trillion-dollars-by-2027
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** direct-funding-models, platform-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** Goldman Sachs estimates the total addressable market for the creator economy at $250 billion in 2025, growing to $480 billion by 2027. Only about 4% of global creators earn more than $100,000 annually, with brand deals constituting roughly 70% of revenue. Useful for scale and growth data, though its framing is oriented toward investor opportunity rather than creator welfare.

### Bleier, Fossen, and Shapira — On the Role of Social Media Platforms in the Creator Economy (2024)

- **Author:** Alexander Bleier, Beth L. Fossen, Michal Shapira
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167811624000545
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** direct-funding-models, platform-economics
- **Access:** paywalled
- **Annotation:** A peer-reviewed study in the International Journal of Research in Marketing that uses literature review and expert interviews to map the roles platforms play in the creator economy: connecting actors, supporting content creation, and facilitating monetisation. Provides a useful academic framework for understanding creator-platform dependencies and the structural power platforms hold over funding flows.

### OECD — Platform Cooperatives and Employment (2023)

- **Author:** OECD
- **Year:** 2023
- **URL:** https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/platform-cooperatives-and-employment_3eab339f-en.html
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** direct-funding-models, platform-economics
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** An OECD working paper providing the most comprehensive institutional overview of platform cooperatives as employment alternatives to conventional gig platforms. Documents working conditions, challenges in scaling, and policy recommendations. Important because it moves beyond individual patronage models to examine structurally different ownership forms for digital platforms.

## Open Source Funding

How does open source funding (or lack of it) affect software infrastructure?

### Hoffmann, Nagle, and Zhou — The Value of Open Source Software (2024)

- **Author:** Manuel Hoffmann, Frank Nagle, Yanuo Zhou
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=65230
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** open-source-funding
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** A Harvard Business School study estimating that firms would need to spend $8.8 trillion to replace existing open source software — roughly 3.5 times their current software spending. Built on the most complete measurement of OSS usage to date, combining Census II data with BuiltWith scans of 9 million company websites. The single most striking quantification of how much economic value rests on largely uncompensated open source labour.

### Linux Foundation, GitHub, and Harvard — 2024 Open Source Software Funding Report (2024)

- **Author:** Linux Foundation, GitHub, Harvard University
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.linuxfoundation.org/research/open-source-funding-2024
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** open-source-funding
- **Access:** registration
- **Annotation:** The most comprehensive survey of how organisations fund open source, finding that responding organisations collectively invest $1.7 billion annually, with 86% of that value being employee labour rather than direct financial contributions. Only 4% of financial contributions go directly to maintainers. Makes visible the enormous gap between the economic value open source creates and the resources directed to sustaining it.

### Przymus and Durieux — Wolves in the Repository: The XZ Utils Supply Chain Attack (2025)

- **Author:** Piotr Przymus, Thomas Durieux
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.17473
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** open-source-funding
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** A detailed forensic analysis of the XZ Utils backdoor (CVE-2024-3094), in which an attacker spent 2.6 years building trust in an under-resourced open source project before injecting a critical backdoor into Linux compression infrastructure. Presented at MSR 2025. Makes the abstract problem of open source underfunding viscerally concrete: this is what happens when critical infrastructure depends on overstretched volunteer maintainers.

### Eghbal — Roads and Bridges: The Unseen Labor Behind Our Digital Infrastructure (2016)

- **Author:** Nadia Eghbal
- **Year:** 2016
- **URL:** https://www.fordfoundation.org/learning/library/research-reports/roads-and-bridges-the-unseen-labor-behind-our-digital-infrastructure/
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** open-source-funding
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** The foundational report that framed open source software as public infrastructure. Commissioned by the Ford Foundation, it argued that digital infrastructure should be treated as a public good and that money alone would not solve sustainability problems without understanding open source culture. A decade on, the problems it identified have only intensified, making it essential historical context for everything that followed.

### Linux Foundation / Harvard / OpenSSF — Census III of Free and Open Source Software (2024)

- **Author:** Frank Nagle, Kate Powell, Richie Zitomer, David A. Wheeler
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.linuxfoundation.org/press/open-source-usage-trends-and-security-challenges-revealed-in-new-study
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** open-source-funding
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** The most comprehensive measurement of which open source libraries are actually used in production, based on over 12 million observations across more than 10,000 companies. Confirms that much of the most widely used open source software is maintained by only a handful of contributors. Essential empirical foundation for understanding where open source infrastructure is most vulnerable to the sustainability gaps documented by Hoffmann, Nagle, and Zhou.

## Platform Economics and Governance

Creator income distribution, platform governance, platform decay.

### Maffie and Hurtado — The Enshittification of Work (2026)

- **Author:** Michael D. Maffie, Hector Hurtado
- **Year:** 2026
- **URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/bjir.70004
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** platform-economics
- **Access:** paywalled
- **Annotation:** Published in the British Journal of Industrial Relations, this peer-reviewed study extends the concept of enshittification from social media platforms to labour conditions. Based on 30 interviews with long-term gig workers in ride-hail and grocery delivery, it identifies three mechanisms of value extraction over time: burden shifting, feature alteration, and market manipulation. Moves the enshittification framework from polemic to empirical labour research.

### Ardoline and Lenzo — The Cognitive and Moral Harms of Platform Decay (2025)

- **Author:** Michael J. Ardoline, Edward Lenzo
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10676-025-09846-1
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** platform-economics, attention-economics
- **Access:** paywalled
- **Annotation:** A philosophy paper in Ethics and Information Technology that argues platform decay is not merely an inconvenience but constitutes genuine cognitive and moral harm. Using the extended mind framework, the authors show that when platforms degrade, they damage users' cognitive capacities and undermine the conditions for developing virtuous character. A rigorous philosophical complement to the more empirical and polemic accounts of platform decline.

## Ad-Driven Wellbeing

How does ad-driven optimisation affect individual wellbeing and behaviour?

### Office of the U.S. Surgeon General — Social Media and Youth Mental Health (2023)

- **Author:** Office of the U.S. Surgeon General
- **Year:** 2023
- **URL:** https://www.hhs.gov/surgeongeneral/reports-and-publications/youth-mental-health/social-media/index.html
- **Type:** report
- **Topics:** ad-driven-wellbeing
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** The most high-profile institutional statement on social media and youth wellbeing, finding that teens spending more than three hours daily on social media face double the risk of depression and anxiety. Important not as the final word on the science — the evidence is more nuanced than the advisory suggests — but as the document that shifted the policy conversation. Best read alongside Vuorre and Przybylski's counterbalancing research.

### Vuorre and Przybylski — Global Well-Being and Mental Health in the Internet Age (2024)

- **Author:** Matti Vuorre, Andrew K. Przybylski
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/21677026231207791
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** ad-driven-wellbeing
- **Access:** paywalled
- **Annotation:** A large-scale study in Clinical Psychological Science analysing data from 2.4 million people across 168 countries over two decades. Finds only small and inconsistent changes in global wellbeing and mental health, with no consistent link to internet and mobile broadband adoption. The most important counterweight to simplistic "social media is destroying mental health" narratives. Does not dismiss harm, but insists on precision about its scale and nature.

### Haidt — The Anxious Generation (2024)

- **Author:** Jonathan Haidt
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://jonathanhaidt.com/anxious-generation/
- **Type:** book
- **Topics:** ad-driven-wellbeing, attention-economics
- **Access:** paywalled
- **Annotation:** The bestselling book that made the case — drawing on observational and experimental studies — that smartphones and social media have driven a rise in youth anxiety and depression. Strongest where it catalogues mechanisms of harm (sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, social comparison) and weakest where it claims a single causal story. Valuable as the most influential popular synthesis, but should be read alongside Vuorre and Przybylski's more cautious findings.

### van Olst, Hendriks, and Smit — The Impact of Social Media Use Interventions on Mental Well-Being (2023)

- **Author:** Mariek M. P. van Olst, Hanneke Hendriks, Eline S. Smit
- **Year:** 2023
- **URL:** https://www.jmir.org/2023/1/e44922
- **Type:** research
- **Topics:** ad-driven-wellbeing
- **Access:** open
- **Annotation:** A systematic review published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research examining whether interventions that change social media use actually improve mental wellbeing. Finds that interventions are effective, especially for depression and when therapy-based approaches are used. Practically useful because it moves beyond correlation to test whether changing behaviour changes outcomes — the question that matters most for a site encouraging people to take action.
