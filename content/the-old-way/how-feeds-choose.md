title: "How Feeds Choose for You"

Open any social media app and you will see a feed. It looks like a stream of things people posted, roughly in order. It is not.

What you see is a ranking. Every post, video, and link has been scored by a system that predicts how likely you are to engage with it — to tap, to comment, to share, to keep watching. The posts that score highest appear first. The ones that score lowest disappear, even if the person who posted them intended you to see them. {{< sidenote >}}An algorithm is just a set of rules for making decisions. Your email spam filter is an algorithm. The difference is that a spam filter is designed to help you. A feed algorithm is designed to help the platform.{{< /sidenote >}}

This ranking is not optional. You cannot turn it off on most platforms. And it is not designed to show you the most accurate, most useful, or most important content. It is designed to keep you on the screen for as long as possible, because time on screen is what gets sold to advertisers.

## Engagement is not enjoyment

The ranking optimises for engagement — taps, likes, shares, comments, watch time. But engagement is not the same as enjoyment, and it is definitely not the same as value.

Content that provokes outrage, anxiety, or tribal loyalty generates more engagement than content that calmly informs. An algorithmic audit of Twitter/X found that its engagement-based ranking amplifies emotionally charged, hostile content significantly more than a simple reverse-chronological feed would. {{< cite "milli-engagement-amplification-2025" >}} The same study found something striking: users themselves reported wanting to see *less* of the divisive content that the algorithm was showing them *more* of. The system was not giving people what they wanted. It was giving them what kept them looking.

This is not a design flaw. It is a design choice. Engagement is the metric because engagement is what sells advertising. The business model and the ranking system are the same thing.

A separate study of 2.7 million posts on Twitter found that tweets sharing information from low-credibility sources performed better in the algorithmic feed than those from credible sources, with the effect concentrated among high-engagement, high-follower accounts. {{< cite "corsi-twitter-amplification-2024" >}} The algorithm does not know what is true. It knows what gets clicks.

Research on Facebook's ranking system found a fundamental trade-off: weighting engagement signals like likes and shares increases platform engagement but also increases misinformation and polarisation. {{< cite "germano-ranking-engagement-2025" >}} Facebook's own 2018 "Meaningful Social Interactions" update, which prioritised content from friends and family, contributed to increased ideological extremism — not because the intention was bad, but because the metric was wrong.

## An old pattern in new clothes

This is not a new problem. The pattern — free content subsidised by advertising, then advertising reshaping the content — is as old as mass media. Tim Wu traces it from the penny newspaper of the 1830s through radio, television, and into the internet age. {{< cite "wu-attention-merchants-2016" >}} Every new medium starts by offering something genuinely useful to attract an audience, then gradually turns that audience into a product for advertisers. The internet is not an exception. It is the latest iteration.

What makes the internet version distinctive is scale and speed. A newspaper editor made choices about what went on the front page. A television producer decided what aired at prime time. These were imperfect, sometimes corrupt, but they were human decisions made by identifiable people. An algorithmic feed makes millions of ranking decisions per second, for each individual user, with no human oversight of any individual result. The incentive is the same — attract eyeballs for advertisers — but the mechanism is automated and personalised in ways that print and broadcast never were.

## What the evidence does not show

It is important to be precise about what the evidence does and does not support. The story is sometimes told as "algorithms are brainwashing you" or "filter bubbles are polarising the world." The research is more specific than that.

Four experiments with nearly 9,000 participants found that manipulating YouTube's recommendations to create filter bubbles and rabbit holes had no detectable short-term effect on political attitudes. {{< cite "liu-filter-bubble-2025" >}} This does not mean algorithmic amplification is harmless — the studies on emotional content and misinformation are robust. But it does mean the effects are more specific and more structural than the broadest claims suggest. The problem is not that algorithms change what you believe overnight. It is that they change what you see, what gets made, and what gets funded — over years, across millions of people, one ranking decision at a time. {{< sidenote >}}For the full research on how algorithmic feeds affect information quality, see [the evidence base](/the-evidence/).{{< /sidenote >}}

That is the mechanism. The feed decides what you see. But the effects do not stop with the feed. The same system shapes what gets made in the first place.
