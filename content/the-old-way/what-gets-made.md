A local newspaper earns a fraction of a penny per page view from digital advertising. To pay one journalist's salary, it needs millions of views every month. That changes what gets written — not because of bad intentions, but because of basic arithmetic.

When advertising is the revenue model, the thing being sold is not the content. It is the audience's attention. Content becomes a means of harvesting eyeballs, and the content that harvests the most eyeballs wins. This produces a predictable result: cheap-to-produce, easy-to-share content crowds out expensive, important content. Not because anyone decided it should, but because the economics demand it.

## The collapse of local news

The most visible casualty is local journalism. The United States now has 213 counties with no local news source at all — news deserts where no one is covering the school board, the water treatment plant, the local court. In a single recent year, 136 newspapers closed. Web traffic to the 100 largest newspapers plunged 45 per cent over four years. {{< cite "medill-local-news-2025" >}}

The financial picture explains why. Print advertising revenue — the money that once paid for newsrooms — fell from roughly $49 billion in 2006 to a small fraction of that. Digital advertising was supposed to replace it. It never did, and it never came close. {{< cite "pew-newspapers-2024" >}} The gap between what print advertising once provided and what digital advertising delivers is the gap in which local journalism is dying.

This is not just an American story. Globally, print circulation and advertising now account for less than half of publisher revenue for the first time in history. {{< cite "wanifra-press-trends-2025" >}} The shift is structural, not cyclical.

There is a countertrend: over 300 digital news startups have launched in five years. But they are overwhelmingly concentrated in metro areas with educated, affluent populations — the same places that already have coverage. The news deserts are not being filled. {{< cite "medill-local-news-2025" >}}

## The creator economy's arithmetic

The pattern extends beyond journalism. The "creator economy" — the ecosystem of independent writers, podcasters, video makers, and artists — is often described as a $250 billion market on its way to $480 billion by 2027. {{< cite "goldmansachs-creator-economy-2025" >}} Those numbers are real, but they obscure what happens inside them.

Of the roughly 8.9 million independent content creators in the United States, 71 per cent earn under $30,000 a year. Only 9 per cent earn more than $100,000. A third make less than $5,000. {{< cite "mbo-creator-economy-2024" >}} The creator economy is a real economy, but for most of the people in it, "creator" is a side project subsidised by something else — a day job, a partner's income, savings.

Brand deals account for roughly 70 per cent of creator revenue, which means most creator income depends on the same advertising system that funds the platforms. {{< cite "goldmansachs-creator-economy-2025" >}} The creator is not an alternative to the ad model. The creator is usually working within it. {{< sidenote >}}Television still dominates news consumption at a ratio of more than five to one over online sources, which complicates the narrative that algorithmic feeds have replaced traditional media. The shift is real but not as complete as it is sometimes described. {{< cite "yang-platforms-news-2025" >}}{{< /sidenote >}}

## Trained to expect free

Only 17 per cent of people globally say they are willing to pay for online news. Fifty-seven per cent say they will not consider paying anything. Among the non-payers, just 2 per cent say they would pay the price of a full subscription. {{< cite "reuters-digital-news-2024" >}}

This is not because people do not value journalism. It is because three decades of ad-supported internet have trained everyone to expect content for free. The price of a newspaper used to be built into daily life. The price of online news feels like an imposition, because the ad model taught people that content arrives without cost. {{< cite "reuters-digital-news-2025" >}}

The result is a squeeze from both sides. Advertising revenue collapses. Willingness to pay remains low. What survives is whatever can be produced cheaply enough to sustain itself on fractions of pennies per view — or whatever is funded by someone with a reason to fund it that has nothing to do with its quality. {{< sidenote >}}The Pew data on newspaper revenue decline is worth sitting with: from $49 billion in print advertising in 2006 to a small fraction of that, with digital advertising never coming close to closing the gap. That is the arithmetic that reshaped what gets written. {{< cite "pew-newspapers-2024" >}}{{< /sidenote >}}

The system shapes what gets made. It also shapes what happens to you when you use it — though perhaps not in the way you have been told.
