## {{< icon "list-filter" >}} How Feeds Choose for You {#how-feeds-choose}

Open any social media app and you will see a feed. It looks like a stream of things people posted, roughly in order. It is not.

Every post, video, and link has been scored by a system predicting how likely you are to tap, comment, share, or keep watching. The posts most likely to keep you there make it to your feed. The rest quietly do not. On most platforms, you cannot change this.

### Engagement is not enjoyment

The feed optimises for engagement: taps, likes, shares, watch time. Engagement is not the same as enjoyment, and it is not the same as value.

An audit of Twitter/X found that engagement-based ranking amplifies emotionally charged, hostile content well beyond what a simple chronological feed would. Users themselves reported wanting to see *less* of the divisive content the algorithm was showing them *more* of. {{< cite "milli-engagement-amplification-2025" >}}{{< cite "corsi-twitter-amplification-2024" >}}

The algorithm is not optimising for what is true, or for what is good for you. It is optimising for what holds your attention.

### An old pattern in new clothes

The pattern is as old as the penny press of the 1830s, with advertising growing up alongside industrialization. Manufacturers needed to create demand for the things they mass produced. Newspapers, then radio, and then television attracted audiences with useful or engaging content, and then sold the audience to advertisers. {{< cite "wu-attention-merchants-2016" >}}

The internet adopted, amplified, and optimized this system. Your feed is tuned uniquely to you, adjusted moment by moment, by systems that guarantee advertising clicks regardless of what is being sold.

## {{< icon "newspaper" >}} What Gets Made (and What Does Not) {#what-gets-made}

In an ad-based model, an influencer, content creator, or newspaper earns fractions of a penny per interaction. At first, this arithmetic reshaped what got written. Then, it decided which voices were amplified and which died out.

### The collapse of local news

The United States now has 213 counties with no local news source at all. No one covering the school board, the water treatment plant, the local court. Print revenue collapsed, digital advertising never replaced it, and AI has accelerated the remaining traffic losses. {{< cite "medill-local-news-2025" >}}{{< cite "pew-newspapers-2024" >}}

### The creator economy's arithmetic

On influencer platforms, roughly 70% of creator revenue comes from brand deals. {{< cite "goldmansachs-creator-economy-2025" >}} Independence is a look, not a structure. The better someone can hold attention, trigger reaction, and stay visible in the feed, the more valuable they become to advertisers. A few win big. Most supply the platform with cheap, abundant labour.

### Trained to expect free

Three decades of ad-supported internet have trained us to expect content for nothing. Only 17% of people globally will pay for online news, and among the 57% who refuse outright, only 2% would pay the price of a subscription even hypothetically. {{< cite "reuters-digital-news-2024" >}}

So the squeeze closes from both sides. Advertising revenue falls. Willingness to pay stays low. What survives is whatever can be made cheaply enough, and perform virally enough, to live on fractions of pennies... or whatever someone wealthy enough is motivated to put in front of you.

## {{< icon "hourglass" >}} What It Costs You {#what-it-costs}

How you allocate your time and attention shapes your life, and time spent in the feed alters relationships, attitudes, and goals.

Think of the last app you opened without really deciding to, just to check updates, and lost more than 30 minutes. That is not a failure of willpower. It is the product working as designed.

Most companies shaping the feed aren't incentivized to help you create strong relationships, develop healthy attitudes, or achieve your personal goals. 

Their job is to keep you engaged. Because when you're engaged, you'll work for free: providing ad views, virality signal, and more custom content for the feed.

## {{< icon "repeat" >}} How the System Sustains Itself {#how-it-sustains-itself}

Each part of this system is doing something locally rational. Platforms maximise engagement because advertisers pay for it. Creators chase engagement because the algorithm rewards it. People stay because the content is compelling and everyone they know is already there. The result is a system almost nobody is happy with, and almost nobody can change alone.

### The engagement is real

Scrolling a feed uses the same mechanism as a slot machine. Most posts are forgettable, but sometimes something lands, and you cannot predict which. {{< cite "sharpe-dopamine-scrolling-2025" >}} That unpredictability is the point. It is what keeps you pulling the lever.

### The enshittification cycle

Cory Doctorow named the pattern in 2023. {{< cite "doctorow-enshittification-2023" >}} A commercial platform launches and offers value to attract users. Once they are locked in (your friends and content are there, and you've your habits built around it), the platform redirects value away from users and toward advertisers, and then toward shareholders. Features degrade. Ads multiply. The feed gets noisier.

### The lock-in is social

Nobody stays because the software is good. They stay because the people they know are there. Group chats, professional contacts, and community spaces are all inside. You may want to move, but everyone you know would have to move too, and they won't. That coordination problem is more powerful than any technical barrier, which is why platforms invest so heavily in features that deepen social investment.

### Beyond social media

The same pattern of "attract, lock in, extract" extends beyond social media. Ride-sharing and delivery platforms follow the identical trajectory: generous early conditions, then, once workers depend on the platform for their livelihood, conditions steadily worsen. {{< cite "maffie-enshittification-work-2026" >}} The logic applies to any business that grows by subsidising one side of a market.

## {{< icon "eye" >}} Seeing Clearly {#seeing-clearly}

We have access to more people, more information, and more tools than any previous generation. New technologies have made this abundance possible, and it should be a good thing. Unfortunately, most of us experience these capabilities through businesses that aren't incentivized to help us become who we want to be.

Seeing things clearly helps us make better choices. We can switch off, touch grass, and just... be still. We can also find ways to take advantage of these amazing capabilities in a way that aligns with our values our goals.
