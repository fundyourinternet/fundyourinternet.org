Most of what you see online is chosen for you. Not by editors, not by your friends, not by the people who made it — by systems designed to keep you looking at the screen for as long as possible. That is not a conspiracy. It is a business model, and it shapes everything. {{< cite "doctorow-enshittification-2023" >}}

This section explains, in plain terms, how that model works and what it costs — not in money, but in the quality of what gets made and the attention you spend on it. It is grounded in research, honest about what the evidence does and does not show, and written for people who would rather understand a system than be told how to feel about it.

{{< outline >}}

## {{< icon "list-filter" >}} How Feeds Choose for You {#how-feeds-choose}

Open any social media app and you will see a feed. It looks like a stream of things people posted, roughly in order. It is not.

What you see is a ranking. Every post, video, and link has been scored by a system that predicts how likely you are to engage with it — to tap, to comment, to share, to keep watching. The posts that score highest appear first. The ones that score lowest disappear, even if the person who posted them intended you to see them. {{< sidenote >}}An algorithm is just a set of rules for making decisions. Your email spam filter is an algorithm. The difference is that a spam filter is designed to help you. A feed algorithm is designed to help the platform.{{< /sidenote >}}

This ranking is not optional. You cannot turn it off on most platforms. And it is not designed to show you the most accurate, most useful, or most important content. It is designed to keep you on the screen for as long as possible, because time on screen is what gets sold to advertisers.

### Engagement is not enjoyment

The ranking optimises for engagement — taps, likes, shares, comments, watch time. But engagement is not the same as enjoyment, and it is definitely not the same as value.

Content that provokes outrage, anxiety, or tribal loyalty generates more engagement than content that calmly informs. An algorithmic audit of Twitter/X found that its engagement-based ranking amplifies emotionally charged, hostile content significantly more than a simple reverse-chronological feed would. {{< cite "milli-engagement-amplification-2025" >}} The same study found something striking: users themselves reported wanting to see *less* of the divisive content that the algorithm was showing them *more* of. The system was not giving people what they wanted. It was giving them what kept them looking.

This is not a design flaw. It is a design choice. Engagement is the metric because engagement is what sells advertising. The business model and the ranking system are the same thing.

A separate study of 2.7 million posts on Twitter found that tweets sharing information from low-credibility sources performed better in the algorithmic feed than those from credible sources, with the effect concentrated among high-engagement, high-follower accounts. {{< cite "corsi-twitter-amplification-2024" >}} The algorithm does not know what is true. It knows what gets clicks.

Research on Facebook's ranking system found a fundamental trade-off: weighting engagement signals like likes and shares increases platform engagement but also increases misinformation and polarisation. {{< cite "germano-ranking-engagement-2025" >}} Facebook's own 2018 "Meaningful Social Interactions" update, which prioritised content from friends and family, contributed to increased ideological extremism — not because the intention was bad, but because the metric was wrong.

### An old pattern in new clothes

This is not a new problem. The pattern — free content subsidised by advertising, then advertising reshaping the content — is as old as mass media. Tim Wu traces it from the penny newspaper of the 1830s through radio, television, and into the internet age. {{< cite "wu-attention-merchants-2016" >}} Every new medium starts by offering something genuinely useful to attract an audience, then gradually turns that audience into a product for advertisers. The internet is not an exception. It is the latest iteration.

What makes the internet version distinctive is scale and speed. A newspaper editor made choices about what went on the front page. A television producer decided what aired at prime time. These were imperfect, sometimes corrupt, but they were human decisions made by identifiable people. An algorithmic feed makes millions of ranking decisions per second, for each individual user, with no human oversight of any individual result. The incentive is the same — attract eyeballs for advertisers — but the mechanism is automated and personalised in ways that print and broadcast never were.

### What the evidence does not show

It is important to be precise about what the evidence does and does not support. The story is sometimes told as "algorithms are brainwashing you" or "filter bubbles are polarising the world." The research is more specific than that.

Four experiments with nearly 9,000 participants found that manipulating YouTube's recommendations to create filter bubbles and rabbit holes had no detectable short-term effect on political attitudes. {{< cite "liu-filter-bubble-2025" >}} This does not mean algorithmic amplification is harmless — the studies on emotional content and misinformation are robust. But it does mean the effects are more specific and more structural than the broadest claims suggest. The problem is not that algorithms change what you believe overnight. It is that they change what you see, what gets made, and what gets funded — over years, across millions of people, one ranking decision at a time. {{< sidenote >}}For the full research on how algorithmic feeds affect information quality, see [the evidence base](/the-evidence/).{{< /sidenote >}}

## {{< icon "newspaper" >}} What Gets Made (and What Does Not) {#what-gets-made}

A local newspaper earns a fraction of a penny per page view from digital advertising. To pay one journalist's salary, it needs millions of views every month. That changes what gets written — not because of bad intentions, but because of basic arithmetic.

When advertising is the revenue model, the thing being sold is not the content. It is the audience's attention. Content becomes a means of harvesting eyeballs, and the content that harvests the most eyeballs wins. This produces a predictable result: cheap-to-produce, easy-to-share content crowds out expensive, important content. Not because anyone decided it should, but because the economics demand it.

### The collapse of local news

The most visible casualty is local journalism. The United States now has 213 counties with no local news source at all — news deserts where no one is covering the school board, the water treatment plant, the local court. In a single recent year, 136 newspapers closed. Web traffic to the 100 largest newspapers plunged 45 per cent over four years. {{< cite "medill-local-news-2025" >}}

The financial picture explains why. Print advertising revenue — the money that once paid for newsrooms — fell from roughly $49 billion in 2006 to a small fraction of that. Digital advertising was supposed to replace it. It never did, and it never came close. {{< cite "pew-newspapers-2024" >}} The gap between what print advertising once provided and what digital advertising delivers is the gap in which local journalism is dying.

This is not just an American story. Globally, print circulation and advertising now account for less than half of publisher revenue for the first time in history. {{< cite "wanifra-press-trends-2025" >}} The shift is structural, not cyclical.

There is a countertrend: over 300 digital news startups have launched in five years. But they are overwhelmingly concentrated in metro areas with educated, affluent populations — the same places that already have coverage. The news deserts are not being filled. {{< cite "medill-local-news-2025" >}}

### The creator economy's arithmetic

The pattern extends beyond journalism. The "creator economy" — the ecosystem of independent writers, podcasters, video makers, and artists — is often described as a $250 billion market on its way to $480 billion by 2027. {{< cite "goldmansachs-creator-economy-2025" >}} Those numbers are real, but they obscure what happens inside them.

Of the roughly 8.9 million independent content creators in the United States, 71 per cent earn under $30,000 a year. Only 9 per cent earn more than $100,000. A third make less than $5,000. {{< cite "mbo-creator-economy-2024" >}} The creator economy is a real economy, but for most of the people in it, "creator" is a side project subsidised by something else — a day job, a partner's income, savings.

Brand deals account for roughly 70 per cent of creator revenue, which means most creator income depends on the same advertising system that funds the platforms. {{< cite "goldmansachs-creator-economy-2025" >}} The creator is not an alternative to the ad model. The creator is usually working within it. {{< sidenote >}}Television still dominates news consumption at a ratio of more than five to one over online sources, which complicates the narrative that algorithmic feeds have replaced traditional media. The shift is real but not as complete as it is sometimes described. {{< cite "yang-platforms-news-2025" >}}{{< /sidenote >}}

### Trained to expect free

Only 17 per cent of people globally say they are willing to pay for online news. Fifty-seven per cent say they will not consider paying anything. Among the non-payers, just 2 per cent say they would pay the price of a full subscription. {{< cite "reuters-digital-news-2024" >}}

This is not because people do not value journalism. It is because three decades of ad-supported internet have trained everyone to expect content for free. The price of a newspaper used to be built into daily life. The price of online news feels like an imposition, because the ad model taught people that content arrives without cost. {{< cite "reuters-digital-news-2025" >}}

The result is a squeeze from both sides. Advertising revenue collapses. Willingness to pay remains low. What survives is whatever can be produced cheaply enough to sustain itself on fractions of pennies per view — or whatever is funded by someone with a reason to fund it that has nothing to do with its quality. {{< sidenote >}}The Pew data on newspaper revenue decline is worth sitting with: from $49 billion in print advertising in 2006 to a small fraction of that, with digital advertising never coming close to closing the gap. That is the arithmetic that reshaped what gets written. {{< cite "pew-newspapers-2024" >}}{{< /sidenote >}}

## {{< icon "hourglass" >}} What It Costs You {#what-it-costs}

You have probably heard some version of the claim that social media is destroying your mental health. The truth is more complicated, and more interesting, than that.

### The headline claim

The most high-profile institutional statement came from the U.S. Surgeon General in 2023: teenagers spending more than three hours a day on social media face double the risk of depression and anxiety symptoms. {{< cite "surgeon-general-social-media-2023" >}} Jonathan Haidt's bestselling book *The Anxious Generation* catalogued the mechanisms — sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, social comparison — and argued that smartphones and social media have driven a generational mental health crisis. {{< cite "haidt-anxious-generation-2024" >}}

These are serious claims from serious people, and the mechanisms they describe are real. But the picture is not as simple as the headlines suggest.

### The counterweight

A study of 2.4 million people across 168 countries over two decades found only small and inconsistent changes in global wellbeing and mental health — with no consistent link to internet and mobile broadband adoption. {{< cite "vuorre-wellbeing-internet-2024" >}} This is the most comprehensive dataset available, and it does not support the story that the internet is straightforwardly destroying mental health. {{< sidenote >}}Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* is the most influential popular account of social media's effect on young people. Vuorre and Przybylski's large-scale study is the most important counterweight. Both are in [the evidence library](/the-evidence/). Reading them together is more useful than reading either alone.{{< /sidenote >}}

The truth is not "social media is fine." It is that the effects are real but more specific and more structural than the broadest claims suggest. The research points most clearly not to a wholesale mental health catastrophe but to something more precise: the systematic capture of attention.

### Where the evidence is clearest

The mechanism is not mysterious. Think of a slot machine. You pull the lever not because every pull pays out, but because some pulls do and you cannot predict which. That unpredictable reward is what keeps you playing. Scrolling a feed works the same way. Most posts are forgettable. But sometimes you find something genuinely good, and that variable reinforcement is what keeps you scrolling. {{< cite "sharpe-dopamine-scrolling-2025" >}}

This is distinct from doom-scrolling — scrolling out of anxiety or compulsion. Dopamine-scrolling is driven by the pursuit of novel, rewarding content, powered by the same reward-mechanism design that makes gambling compelling. It is not a character flaw. It is a design pattern, engineered into the product.

Attention is the real currency of the ad-supported internet — and not just metaphorically. Follower counts, engagement metrics, view totals: these are forms of stored attention that platforms can monetise and redistribute. {{< cite "heitmayer-attention-economics-2025" >}} When people talk about the "attention economy," they are describing a system in which your capacity to focus has been turned into a commodity that is bought and sold without your involvement in the transaction.

### Systemic, not personal

Johann Hari's *Stolen Focus* identifies twelve structural causes of the modern attention crisis, from the design of technology to pollution and diet. His central argument is that attention collapse is systemic, not a personal failing — and that the solutions need to be collective, not individual. {{< cite "hari-stolen-focus-2022" >}}

That is true, and it matters. But it is not the whole story.

A systematic review of interventions that change social media use found that they do improve mental wellbeing, particularly for depression — and that therapy-based approaches are especially effective. {{< cite "vanolst-social-media-interventions-2023" >}} Individual action is not pointless. It is just not sufficient on its own. The structural problems need structural responses, and the personal costs are real enough to warrant personal awareness.

### A tool that gets worse

There is one more cost worth naming, and it is perhaps the most quietly corrosive. When a platform you rely on starts to degrade — more ads, worse recommendations, features removed, communities fragmented — that degradation is not just an annoyance. If you have built habits, relationships, and information routines around that platform, its decline damages your cognitive infrastructure. {{< cite "ardoline-platform-decay-2025" >}}

This is the cost that is hardest to see, because it happens gradually. The platform you joined five years ago is not the platform you use today. It has been slowly restructured to extract more value from you and give less back. You may not have consciously noticed, but your experience has changed — more noise, less signal, more effort required to find the things that matter to you.

## {{< icon "repeat" >}} How the System Sustains Itself {#how-it-sustains-itself}

Nobody planned this. There is no committee deciding to make the internet worse. The system sustains itself because every part of it is doing something locally rational.

The platform maximises engagement, because that is what advertisers pay for. The advertiser buys attention, because that is the cheapest way to reach people at scale. The creator chases engagement, because that is what the platform's algorithm rewards. And you stay, because the content is engineered to be compelling and because everyone you know is already there.

Each of those decisions makes sense in isolation. Together, they produce a system that nobody specifically chose and that almost nobody is fully happy with — but that persists anyway, because no single participant can unilaterally change it.

### The enshittification cycle

Cory Doctorow named this pattern in 2023. {{< cite "doctorow-enshittification-2023" >}} It goes like this: a platform launches and offers genuine value to attract users. It works well, people join, and the platform grows. Then, once the users are locked in — their friends are there, their content is there, their habits are built around it — the platform begins redirecting value away from users and toward advertisers and shareholders. Features degrade. Ads multiply. The feed gets noisier. The things that made the platform worth joining in the first place are quietly hollowed out. {{< sidenote >}}The word "enshittification" is crude but precise. It describes the specific process by which platforms that start generous become extractive — not through a sudden change of heart, but through the predictable incentives of venture-funded growth. This site uses the term only where the concept is being explained.{{< /sidenote >}}

This is not evil. It is the predictable result of a business model that requires growth, then monetisation, then returns to investors. The platform that once needed you to love it now only needs you to keep using it. And if you keep using it — even while complaining — the model is working as intended.

### The lock-in is social

You do not stay on a declining platform because the software is good. You stay because the people you know are there. Your group chats, your professional contacts, your community threads, your shared photos — they are all inside the platform, and moving them somewhere else requires everyone to move at once. That coordination problem is the real lock-in, and it is far more powerful than any technical barrier.

This is why platforms can degrade so far without losing users. The cost of leaving is not a subscription fee you stop paying. It is relationships you might lose, conversations you would miss, a network you spent years building. The platform knows this, which is why it invests heavily in features that increase your social investment — and very little in features that would make it easy to leave.

### Beyond social media

The same pattern — attract, lock in, extract — extends far beyond social media. Research based on interviews with long-term gig workers documents how ride-sharing and delivery platforms follow the identical trajectory: early conditions are generous (high pay, flexible schedules, bonus incentives), and then, once workers depend on the platform for their livelihood, conditions steadily worsen through burden shifting, feature changes, and market manipulation. {{< cite "maffie-enshittification-work-2026" >}}

This matters because it shows the pattern is not specific to social media or to advertising. It is a feature of any platform business that grows by subsidising one side of a market and then extracts value once that side is locked in. The ad-supported content platform is one instance. The gig economy platform is another. The underlying logic is the same.

### Not a conspiracy

It is tempting to look at this system and see a villain — some executive in a glass office deciding to make your experience worse. The reality is less dramatic and more structural. Every actor in the system is responding to incentives that make sense from where they sit. The platform responds to its investors. The advertisers respond to their clients. The creators respond to the algorithm. You respond to the feed.

The system does not require your ignorance. It just does not require your awareness. That is the difference between a conspiracy and a business model. A conspiracy needs to be hidden. A business model operates in the open, documented in earnings calls and annual reports, visible to anyone who looks. The reason most people do not look is not that someone is preventing them. It is that the system is designed to be frictionless — to require no thought, no decision, no awareness of the transaction taking place.

But once you do see it — once you understand that the problem is not any one platform but the business model beneath it — something shifts. If the problem is the funding model, then anything funded differently is worth a look. That is not idealism. It is arithmetic. {{< sidenote >}}If you are thinking "so what *is* funded differently?" — that is exactly what [A New Way](/a-new-way/) covers.{{< /sidenote >}}

## {{< icon "eye" >}} Seeing Clearly {#seeing-clearly}

If you have read this far, you now understand something that most people use the internet without ever considering. That understanding is, by itself, worth something.

Here is what you now know. Feeds rank for engagement, not for quality — and the content they amplify is often not the content users actually want to see. That ranking system shapes what gets made, because creators and publishers must chase the metrics the algorithm rewards or lose visibility. The costs to you are real but specific: not a wholesale mental health catastrophe, but a systematic capture of attention through design patterns borrowed from slot machines. And the whole system sustains itself not through conspiracy but through structure — every participant doing something locally rational, producing a collective outcome nobody specifically chose.

None of that is secret. All of it is documented in research, earnings reports, and public statements. The system operates in the open. What it does not do is make itself legible. There is no moment, using any platform, where the system pauses to explain what it is doing and why. You are never asked to consent to the transaction in which your attention is sold. You are simply enrolled in it, by default, the moment you open the app.

### What changes

Knowing this does not mean you stop using the internet. That would be neither practical nor desirable. The internet is full of things worth your time — communities, tools, knowledge, creative work that matters to you. The point is not to withdraw. It is to see the difference between content you chose and content that was chosen for you.

You can notice when you are scrolling from habit rather than interest. You can recognise the pull of variable reinforcement — the "just one more" feeling that has nothing to do with whether the next post will actually be worth your time. You can distinguish between a platform that is serving you and one that is extracting from you.

These are not dramatic changes. They are the kind of small shift in perception that, once made, cannot be unmade. It is like knowing that supermarkets place sweets at the checkout. You still buy sweets sometimes. But you buy them because you decided to, not because a system put them in your path at the moment you were least likely to think about it. The goal is not purity. It is awareness.

### What awareness does not solve

Individual awareness matters. Research shows that interventions which change how people use social media do improve wellbeing, particularly for depression. {{< cite "vanolst-social-media-interventions-2023" >}} Changing your own behaviour is not pointless.

But the structural problems described in this section — the misaligned incentives, the degradation of public-interest content, the extraction cycles of platform capitalism — cannot be solved by individual awareness alone. Systemic problems require systemic responses. {{< cite "hari-stolen-focus-2022" >}} {{< sidenote >}}The front page of this site asks you to pick one thing you rely on online and find out if you can support it. That is the practice version of the understanding this section has outlined. [Start here](/) if you have not already.{{< /sidenote >}}

The good news is that those systemic responses already exist. In dozens of communities — open source, journalism, podcasting, the decentralised web — people are building direct relationships between the people who make things and the people who value them. Not a single platform. Not a movement with a manifesto. A pattern, emerging independently in different places, grounded in a simple premise: if the problem is the business model, try a different business model.
