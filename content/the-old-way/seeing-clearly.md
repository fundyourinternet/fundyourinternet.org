If you have read this far, you now understand something that most people use the internet without ever considering. That understanding is, by itself, worth something.

Here is what you now know. Feeds rank for engagement, not for quality — and the content they amplify is often not the content users actually want to see. That ranking system shapes what gets made, because creators and publishers must chase the metrics the algorithm rewards or lose visibility. The costs to you are real but specific: not a wholesale mental health catastrophe, but a systematic capture of attention through design patterns borrowed from slot machines. And the whole system sustains itself not through conspiracy but through structure — every participant doing something locally rational, producing a collective outcome nobody specifically chose.

None of that is secret. All of it is documented in research, earnings reports, and public statements. The system operates in the open. What it does not do is make itself legible. There is no moment, using any platform, where the system pauses to explain what it is doing and why. You are never asked to consent to the transaction in which your attention is sold. You are simply enrolled in it, by default, the moment you open the app.

## What changes

Knowing this does not mean you stop using the internet. That would be neither practical nor desirable. The internet is full of things worth your time — communities, tools, knowledge, creative work that matters to you. The point is not to withdraw. It is to see the difference between content you chose and content that was chosen for you.

You can notice when you are scrolling from habit rather than interest. You can recognise the pull of variable reinforcement — the "just one more" feeling that has nothing to do with whether the next post will actually be worth your time. You can distinguish between a platform that is serving you and one that is extracting from you.

These are not dramatic changes. They are the kind of small shift in perception that, once made, cannot be unmade. It is like knowing that supermarkets place sweets at the checkout. You still buy sweets sometimes. But you buy them because you decided to, not because a system put them in your path at the moment you were least likely to think about it. The goal is not purity. It is awareness.

## What awareness does not solve

Individual awareness matters. Research shows that interventions which change how people use social media do improve wellbeing, particularly for depression. {{< cite "vanolst-social-media-interventions-2023" >}} Changing your own behaviour is not pointless.

But the structural problems described in this section — the misaligned incentives, the degradation of public-interest content, the extraction cycles of platform capitalism — cannot be solved by individual awareness alone. Systemic problems require systemic responses. {{< cite "hari-stolen-focus-2022" >}} {{< sidenote >}}The front page of this site asks you to pick one thing you rely on online and find out if you can support it. That is the practice version of the understanding this section has outlined. [Start here](/) if you have not already.{{< /sidenote >}}

The good news is that those systemic responses already exist. In dozens of communities — open source, journalism, podcasting, the decentralised web — people are building direct relationships between the people who make things and the people who value them. Not a single platform. Not a movement with a manifesto. A pattern, emerging independently in different places, grounded in a simple premise: if the problem is the business model, try a different business model.
