You have probably heard some version of the claim that social media is destroying your mental health. The truth is more complicated, and more interesting, than that.

## The headline claim

The most high-profile institutional statement came from the U.S. Surgeon General in 2023: teenagers spending more than three hours a day on social media face double the risk of depression and anxiety symptoms. {{< cite "surgeon-general-social-media-2023" >}} Jonathan Haidt's bestselling book *The Anxious Generation* catalogued the mechanisms — sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, social comparison — and argued that smartphones and social media have driven a generational mental health crisis. {{< cite "haidt-anxious-generation-2024" >}}

These are serious claims from serious people, and the mechanisms they describe are real. But the picture is not as simple as the headlines suggest.

## The counterweight

A study of 2.4 million people across 168 countries over two decades found only small and inconsistent changes in global wellbeing and mental health — with no consistent link to internet and mobile broadband adoption. {{< cite "vuorre-wellbeing-internet-2024" >}} This is the most comprehensive dataset available, and it does not support the story that the internet is straightforwardly destroying mental health. {{< sidenote >}}Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* is the most influential popular account of social media's effect on young people. Vuorre and Przybylski's large-scale study is the most important counterweight. Both are in [the evidence library](/the-evidence/). Reading them together is more useful than reading either alone.{{< /sidenote >}}

The truth is not "social media is fine." It is that the effects are real but more specific and more structural than the broadest claims suggest. The research points most clearly not to a wholesale mental health catastrophe but to something more precise: the systematic capture of attention.

## Where the evidence is clearest

The mechanism is not mysterious. Think of a slot machine. You pull the lever not because every pull pays out, but because some pulls do and you cannot predict which. That unpredictable reward is what keeps you playing. Scrolling a feed works the same way. Most posts are forgettable. But sometimes you find something genuinely good, and that variable reinforcement is what keeps you scrolling. {{< cite "sharpe-dopamine-scrolling-2025" >}}

This is distinct from doom-scrolling — scrolling out of anxiety or compulsion. Dopamine-scrolling is driven by the pursuit of novel, rewarding content, powered by the same reward-mechanism design that makes gambling compelling. It is not a character flaw. It is a design pattern, engineered into the product.

Attention is the real currency of the ad-supported internet — and not just metaphorically. Follower counts, engagement metrics, view totals: these are forms of stored attention that platforms can monetise and redistribute. {{< cite "heitmayer-attention-economics-2025" >}} When people talk about the "attention economy," they are describing a system in which your capacity to focus has been turned into a commodity that is bought and sold without your involvement in the transaction.

## Systemic, not personal

Johann Hari's *Stolen Focus* identifies twelve structural causes of the modern attention crisis, from the design of technology to pollution and diet. His central argument is that attention collapse is systemic, not a personal failing — and that the solutions need to be collective, not individual. {{< cite "hari-stolen-focus-2022" >}}

That is true, and it matters. But it is not the whole story.

A systematic review of interventions that change social media use found that they do improve mental wellbeing, particularly for depression — and that therapy-based approaches are especially effective. {{< cite "vanolst-social-media-interventions-2023" >}} Individual action is not pointless. It is just not sufficient on its own. The structural problems need structural responses, and the personal costs are real enough to warrant personal awareness.

## A tool that gets worse

There is one more cost worth naming, and it is perhaps the most quietly corrosive. When a platform you rely on starts to degrade — more ads, worse recommendations, features removed, communities fragmented — that degradation is not just an annoyance. If you have built habits, relationships, and information routines around that platform, its decline damages your cognitive infrastructure. {{< cite "ardoline-platform-decay-2025" >}}

This is the cost that is hardest to see, because it happens gradually. The platform you joined five years ago is not the platform you use today. It has been slowly restructured to extract more value from you and give less back. You may not have consciously noticed, but your experience has changed — more noise, less signal, more effort required to find the things that matter to you.

The costs are real, if more specific than the headlines suggest. But the most important thing about this system is not what it does to you. It is how it sustains itself.
