title: "How the System Sustains Itself"

Nobody planned this. There is no committee deciding to make the internet worse. The system sustains itself because every part of it is doing something locally rational.

The platform maximises engagement, because that is what advertisers pay for. The advertiser buys attention, because that is the cheapest way to reach people at scale. The creator chases engagement, because that is what the platform's algorithm rewards. And you stay, because the content is engineered to be compelling and because everyone you know is already there.

Each of those decisions makes sense in isolation. Together, they produce a system that nobody specifically chose and that almost nobody is fully happy with — but that persists anyway, because no single participant can unilaterally change it.

## The enshittification cycle

Cory Doctorow named this pattern in 2023. {{< cite "doctorow-enshittification-2023" >}} It goes like this: a platform launches and offers genuine value to attract users. It works well, people join, and the platform grows. Then, once the users are locked in — their friends are there, their content is there, their habits are built around it — the platform begins redirecting value away from users and toward advertisers and shareholders. Features degrade. Ads multiply. The feed gets noisier. The things that made the platform worth joining in the first place are quietly hollowed out. {{< sidenote >}}The word "enshittification" is crude but precise. It describes the specific process by which platforms that start generous become extractive — not through a sudden change of heart, but through the predictable incentives of venture-funded growth. This site uses the term only where the concept is being explained.{{< /sidenote >}}

This is not evil. It is the predictable result of a business model that requires growth, then monetisation, then returns to investors. The platform that once needed you to love it now only needs you to keep using it. And if you keep using it — even while complaining — the model is working as intended.

## The lock-in is social

You do not stay on a declining platform because the software is good. You stay because the people you know are there. Your group chats, your professional contacts, your community threads, your shared photos — they are all inside the platform, and moving them somewhere else requires everyone to move at once. That coordination problem is the real lock-in, and it is far more powerful than any technical barrier.

This is why platforms can degrade so far without losing users. The cost of leaving is not a subscription fee you stop paying. It is relationships you might lose, conversations you would miss, a network you spent years building. The platform knows this, which is why it invests heavily in features that increase your social investment — and very little in features that would make it easy to leave.

## Beyond social media

The same pattern — attract, lock in, extract — extends far beyond social media. Research based on interviews with long-term gig workers documents how ride-sharing and delivery platforms follow the identical trajectory: early conditions are generous (high pay, flexible schedules, bonus incentives), and then, once workers depend on the platform for their livelihood, conditions steadily worsen through burden shifting, feature changes, and market manipulation. {{< cite "maffie-enshittification-work-2026" >}}

This matters because it shows the pattern is not specific to social media or to advertising. It is a feature of any platform business that grows by subsidising one side of a market and then extracts value once that side is locked in. The ad-supported content platform is one instance. The gig economy platform is another. The underlying logic is the same.

## Not a conspiracy

It is tempting to look at this system and see a villain — some executive in a glass office deciding to make your experience worse. The reality is less dramatic and more structural. Every actor in the system is responding to incentives that make sense from where they sit. The platform responds to its investors. The advertisers respond to their clients. The creators respond to the algorithm. You respond to the feed.

The system does not require your ignorance. It just does not require your awareness. That is the difference between a conspiracy and a business model. A conspiracy needs to be hidden. A business model operates in the open, documented in earnings calls and annual reports, visible to anyone who looks. The reason most people do not look is not that someone is preventing them. It is that the system is designed to be frictionless — to require no thought, no decision, no awareness of the transaction taking place.

But once you do see it — once you understand that the problem is not any one platform but the business model beneath it — something shifts. If the problem is the funding model, then anything funded differently is worth a look. That is not idealism. It is arithmetic. {{< sidenote >}}If you are thinking "so what *is* funded differently?" — that is exactly what [A New Way](/a-new-way/) covers.{{< /sidenote >}}

Understanding the system is not the same as being stuck in it.
